## 2. Required Disclosures and Statements

### 2.1 Opening / Identity Disclosure (Standard 1)

- **Trigger:** As soon as possible on making contact (first words of the call)
- **Mandatory content:**
  - Agent's full name
  - Company name (Watt)
  - Purpose / reason for the call
- **Canonical example wording (Page 28):**
  `"Good morning/afternoon, my name is [name] and I am calling from Watt. I am contacting you regarding the renewal of your [Supplier Name] energy contract."`
- **Prohibited phrasings (Page 11–12):**
  - `"We get preferential rates that other brokers don't get"` — only permitted if true for ALL retailers; use `"most retailers"` qualifier
  - `"We control …% of the energy market"` — too vague unless publicly documented
  - `"don't worry, I am not trying to get you to change supplier"` — misleading if a switch is later proposed
  - `"We work across Europe"` — must be factually precise

### 2.2 Commission / Remuneration Disclosure (Standard 3g, Page 20)

- **Trigger:** When describing how the company is paid; must be disclosed before or during the pricing discussion
- **Prohibited phrasings:**
  - `"you don't pay anything for this service"`
  - `"we are paid by the supplier"` (alone — omits that commission is embedded in the unit rate)
- **Required compliant alternatives:**
  - `"We receive a commission from the retailer for bringing them this sale, which is then included within the unit rate we have discussed with you. This reflects the service we have provided, and will continue to provide, to you. This service consists of …….."`
  - `"The cost of our service will already be included within our supplier proposals that we present"`

### 2.3 Market Search Scope Disclosure (Standard 3, Pages 16–17)

- **Trigger:** When quoting a "best" rate or describing a market search
- **Rule:** Only use `"best price in the market"` if ALL suppliers were approached; otherwise specify scope
- **Required phrasing if partial search:** `"I have approached some of our main suppliers and this is the best I can get"`
- **Prohibited:** `"I have finalised the search and the most competitive rate is …"` unless full market scope is confirmed; `"I will be sending a tender out to the market"` (implies all suppliers unless qualified)

### 2.4 Price Prediction Disclosures (Pages 17–18)

- **Trigger:** Any forward-looking statement about energy prices
- **Prohibited absolute prediction phrases (Page 17):**
  - `"We will always be in a rising market"`
  - `"The only way is up with the energy market"`
  - `"That is on the basis that the price will rise"`
  - `"Take a 5 year deal to protect you from price rises"` (unqualified)
  - `"The energy market has never gone down"`
  - `"You know yourself that prices always go up"`
  - `"The energy market will be rising until 2016"`
  - `"Prices will be going up by 20% in the next 6 months"` (without substantiation)
  - `"With this 5 year contract because of price rises you will make your money in years 2,3,4 and 5"`
  - `"We are anticipating a 1% increase a month and 36% over the next 3 years"` (without substantiation)
  - `"When you look at these prices again in a year's time I would imagine they would be around 46p"` (without substantiation)
- **Required alternative — must cite evidence/source (Page 18):**
  - `"Based on how the market has gone in the last 5 years, then you should be saving money on this contract by year 2"`
  - `"The latest predictions from 2020–2023 show that prices are expected to rise by 20% in the next 2 years"`
  - `"Looking over the last few years, on average the only way is up for the energy market"`
  - `"Take a 5 year contract to protect you from the price increases that are likely to happen based on what has happened in the market over the last few years"`
- **Rule:** `"Always provide substantiation to any claims you make to the customer about prices in the future."` (Page 18)

### 2.5 Other Supplier / Industry Statements (Page 19)

- **Rule:** Only make statements about other retailers if the information is:
  - In the public domain, OR
  - You can clearly demonstrate how the information was obtained
- **Prohibited:** Using regulatory fines against competitors as a scare tactic unless you understand exactly why the fine was applied

### 2.6 Verbal Script Disclosure Before Contracting (Standard 7, Page 24)

- **Trigger:** Before running the verbal contract script
- **Required explanation:** Customer must be told the script is not just about "locking in prices" — it is **a legally binding verbal contract**
- **Rule:** `"Before you run through the script make sure the customer understands that it is not just about 'locking in the prices', it is actually a legally binding verbal contract."`

### 2.7 Principal Terms Explanation (Standard 5, Page 22)

- **Trigger:** Before any contracting process
- **Required:** Agent must explain the principal terms of the product; customer must clearly express understanding and willingness to proceed

## 5. Vulnerable Customer Rules

### Detection (Page 14)
A customer is vulnerable if there is risk they:
- Cannot fully understand what is being discussed, OR
- Cannot make a decision in their best interests

**Vulnerability indicators to monitor:**
- Language difficulties / non-native speaker confusion
- Illness (stated or inferred)
- Confusion about what is happening in the conversation
- Any "specific situation" affecting the customer at that time

### Required Actions (Page 14)
- `"If the customer shows any signs [of not understanding] then you should ensure you take longer with the customer to make sure they do understand"`
- Offer to call the customer back
- Get support from Manager if in doubt
- `"If in doubt then give the customer some extra time, offer to call them back, get support from your Manager etc."`

### Prohibited Actions (Page 13–14)
- Pushing through a sale when the customer is unclear about what they are saying "yes" to
- Ignoring a customer's clear indication that contact is inconvenient, unwelcome or inappropriate (Standard 2)
- Continuing a call with a demonstrably confused or ill customer without taking additional steps

### Cessation Rule (Standard 2, Page 13)
- If a customer clearly indicates they do not want to continue the conversation at that point OR in the future → after one appropriate attempt to engage, **STOP** — do not continue
- Regulatory basis: Ofgem Licence Conditions

## 7. Letter of Authority (LOA) Rules

### When Required (Standard 8, Pages 25–26)
- Required when a Partner acts on a customer's behalf (e.g., to access billing/meter data, conduct a market search)
- Verbal LOA is accepted **only if:**
  - The verbal LOA script was provided by OR approved by the chosen supplier
  - The Partner follows the same compliance principles as apply to written LOAs

### What an LOA Must Contain / Satisfy (Page 26)
1. Fully legible
2. Signed by the customer (or authorised signatory)
3. Fully completed — all key information present
4. Any post-signature amendments initialled by the customer + re-signed
5. **Photos of LOA are NOT accepted** — scan or original only
6. All information accurate and complete
7. If site address differs from company address — both addresses captured in appropriate fields
8. If Limited Company — details must match Companies House records

### Verbal LOA Conditions (Page 25)
- Script must be supplier-approved
- Same compliance principles as written LOA apply
- No additional verbal LOA-specific confirmation list is given beyond the general Standards

## 10. Severity Tiers

The guide references a **Compliance Standards matrix** (Section 3, Pages 29–37) with graded severity levels. The narrative on Page 29 states:

> `"As a general guide the Levels referred to follow these basic principles:"`

The matrix pages (30–37) contain no extractable text in the source markdown — they appear to be image-only pages in the original PDF. Based on the narrative structure the guide uses "Levels" (not "minor/major/critical" labels explicitly), suggesting a **numbered level system**. The following is confirmed from the text:

| What is confirmed | Source |
|-------------------|--------|
| Severity is graded by "Level" | Page 29 |
| Each Standard (1–8) has failures graded on severity | Page 29 |
| Matrix is the authoritative severity reference | Page 29 |
| The matrix is described as "not an exhaustive list" | Page 29 |

**Inferred tier structure** (from guide language across standards):
- **Level 1 (Minor):** Process lapses unlikely to mislead or cause customer detriment (e.g., slight opening statement variation)
- **Level 2 (Significant):** Information gaps or inaccuracies that could confuse customer but contract remains valid (e.g., partial market search not qualified)
- **Level 3 (Major):** Failures that could render a contract unenforceable or cause direct customer detriment (e.g., script not run, no authority check)
- **Level 4 (Critical):** Fraudulent, coercive, or deliberate misconduct; zero-tolerance breaches (Standard 3d: `"not commit forgery or fraud, or any other unethical or high risk behavior"`)

> **Note:** Exact Level labels and weightings are only in the matrix image pages not captured in the source markdown.
