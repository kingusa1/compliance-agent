Watt.co.uk is a company that places compliance, ethics and quality at the centre of all the activity we undertake. As part of the wider
Watt Group we have a set of business and ethical guidelines, captured in the Watt Code of Conduct that guide all aspects of how we
work and how we act. 
Day to day this manifests itself at Watt in a number of different ways. Specifically in our sales channels however these values can be
seen in our absolute commitment that:
1) All customers who agree to take a Watt product or service have done so on the basis of receiving reliable, accurate and complete
information from the sales person they engaged with about the product or service, and about the other options and considerations
that exist within the business energy market place. 
2) Watt will always try to build long term sustainable relationships with its Sales Partners, with whom we will work collaboratively and
provide as much support and guidance as possible to ensure these relationships generate quality, compliant sales, for the benefit of
our customers, our Partners, and Watt.co.uk 
This document is therefore part of our fulfilment of these commitments, and is an attempt to distil all the information Watt has
gathered from its compliance activities with its Partners into one easy to use reference guide. Watt undertakes a large number of visits,
audits, reviews etc with its Partners and from this activity has identified a lot of different areas we feel our Partners in general could
benefit from knowing about. This document also captures some general rules, guidance and advice on a range of compliant subjects.

programs that stand out and bringing them together in one place to create a best practice guide.
Proactive Controls
1) Recruitment / vetting of Agents  – A robust set of processes to ensure that only high quality
Employees / Agents  are recruited are a key control. Using this stage as a way to eliminate anybody
that does not clearly demonstrate the necessary skills, integrity and knowledge needed saves
potential future pain which can be costly to not only the bottom line but also your reputation. This
is especially true for  Agents where this vital stage provides the opportunity to look at their end to
end compliance controls, integrity and knowledge to build the full picture and assess any risk. The
Partner that holds the direct relationship with the supplier is 100% responsible for all sales
regardless of via their own Employees, self-employed Agents or Sub-sales.

2) Training – Ensuring that all Employees / Agents have the right skills, experience and
knowledge in order to sell effectively, in a quality and compliant way. Getting this area right will
dramatically reduce any potential issues that can occur further down the line. Training is most
effective when it is an on-going program through the term of the Employee / Agents relationship

This is a practical guide to delivering compliant sales that is based on issues found by Watt while
auditing our Agents sales activity. It is written mostly in reference to the Sales Agent interacting with a
customer, and is split into sections based on the Watt Compliance Standards (see Section 3).PRACTICAL SALE COMPLIANCE GUIDE 
Standard 1 - The sales agent will, as soon as possible on making contact, identify themselves, the
business they represent and their purpose reason for the call. The opening of an interaction with a
prospective customer is a risky area as you try and get the customer interested in what you do and what
you can offer. It can be easy to say inappropriate things at this stage. The Opening
Guidance
 You need to be clear about who you are, who you work for and the purpose of the call to be 100% compliant.
‘We get preferential rates that other brokers don't get’ – this is not always the case so you need to be clear
exactly what the situation is e.g. ‘We get preferential rates from most retailers, that other brokers don’t get’.

‘We control…% of the energy market’ – this is too vague. Which bit of the market and is this information in
the public domain?
If you make any sort of claims about your company, its size, the amount of work it does, the areas it works in
etc these need to be accurate. 
 When describing your company ensure that it is factual and relevant – ‘We work across Europe’ could give a
false impression of your size, compared to ‘We work in several European countries and are still expanding’.
Also ask yourself what relevance this information is to the case in hand and if it is not, don't mention it. 
Statements such as ‘don't worry I am not trying to get you to change supplier’ risk making you appear
devious if later in the call you then start talking about getting prices from the market and looking for a better
deal. 
Be very clear about who you are and why you are calling – ‘We are not just trying to get you to change
supplier. We have a range of energy products we can offer including a bill analysis which can indicate
whether you may be able to get a better deal at your current supplier or elsewhere if required’

Comments about comparing the market to get the best price are full of risk around misleading customers as to
what has actually occurred.  
Only tell customers that an offer is the best one in the market if you have gone to all suppliers and compared the whole

 ‘I have finalised the search and the most competitive rate I have got is .....’ – misleading unless you give the customer an

When you are quoting the numbers of suppliers your company work with then ensure it is accurate.  
Statements such as ‘I will be sending a tender out to the market’ implies it will go to all the suppliers. If this is not the case then
be more specific with the statements you make to customers.  
The phrase ‘best price’ is loaded with risk. Exactly what is it better than? More often than not prices TPIs quote customers will

The key question is how do we know that prices will go up? We do not know, but what we can rely on
however is facts, figures and evidence:
‘Based on how the market has gone in the last 5 years, then you should be saving money on this contract by
year 2’ 
‘The latest predictions from 2020 - 2023 show that prices are expected to rise by 20%  in the next 2 years.
 ‘Looking over the last few years, on average the only way is up for the energy market’ 
 ‘Take a 5 year contract to protect you from the price increases that are likely to happen based on what has
happened in the market over the last few years’ 
Always provide substantiation to any claims you make to the customer about prices in the future.
If you try and provide elaborate and in depth explanations to support claims about prices rises, including
about Ukraine, Brexit, nuclear energy etc then you must be 100% accurate with all your facts and
communicate this in a clear and understandable way. Rushing through an explanation like this can risk leaving
customers even more confused and mislead tem if you don't get your facts right.

TALKING ABOUT THE INDUSTRY & SUPPLIERS 
As with price rises if you are making any comments about other retailers you must be
able to substantiate these claims. For example suggesting that another retailer will be
putting their prices up, with no evidence to substantiate this is not compliant. Using
industry news such as fines for other retailers as a scare tactic is also risky unless you
understand exactly what the fine was for and why it was applied. Only talk about other
retailers or industry developments if this information is in the public domain or you

Standard 4 - The sales agent must at all times: 
a. Take appropriate steps to ensure that the customer has the authority to enter into a legally binding supply
contract.
b. Take appropriate steps to ensure that the customer is a non-domestic customer and that the usage is not wholly
or mainly for domestic purposes. 
Reason for this standard: To ensure the legality of the contract being entered into.
Failure to ensure we are contracting with a customer who actually has the authority to enter into a legally binding contract on
the businesses behalf means that there will always be a risk of that contract being proved to have no legal basis. It is therefore
important to check and to pick up on any comments from the customer that suggest another person may actually be the one
needed to agree the contract.
For Watt, which only supplies Business customers, if a domestic customer is transferred to us based on advice from a Partner
then it will cause significant time and effort to resolve the situation satisfactorily, including additional inconvenience for the
customer. It will also risk Watt breaching Ofgem requirements. Please therefore be diligent in assessing whether a customer is
domestic or business, and do not attempt to transfer any customers with Watt that could be classed as domestic. 
In addition, Watt does not have the system functionality to support prepayment meters so any customer you come across with a
prepayment meter cannot be transferred to the suppliers Watt work with.
If an agent arranges a contract with a customer with a prepayment meter it will again cause unnecessary effort, cost and
inconvenience for the customer and Watt.

Standard 7 - The sales agent must adhere to any relevant Verbal Sales Script provided by Watt at all times.
Reason for this standard: Compliance with BPMMRs and Ofgem Licence Conditions , and to ensure the legality of the
contract being entered into.
The  verbal contract sales scripts are vital documents that serve a very important purpose, protecting the customer, TGP,
the TPI and the sales agent involved. Failure to adhere to them creates risk for all parties.
Before you run through the script make sure the customer understands that it is not just about ‘locking in the prices’, it is actually a
legally binding verbal contract. 
Ensure you run through the script in a clear and methodical manor. The script is there to protect Watt, the supplier and the Customer.
Rushing through it, not saying it all, glossing over parts of it, or not giving the customer time to consider what you are saying could
result in the contract being unenforceable. 
If you run through the contract script properly then you have great protection should that customer come back and accuse you of any
unethical behaviour. 
In addition if you don't fully understand some elements of the sales script then ask for guidance because if a customer asks you about
it and you give them an inaccurate answer then it again could have serious consequences. 
Do not answer for the customer. It is important that the customer answers and shows they understand all the questions. 
Make sure you use the right sales script for the right occasion. 
Make sure you have provided the customer with all the facts before you do the script. The script should be a simple recap of everything
the customer already knows and has agreed to.
